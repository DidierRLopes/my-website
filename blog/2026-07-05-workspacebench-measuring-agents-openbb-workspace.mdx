If agents are going to drive this workspace for real analysts, we (OpenBB) need to find a way to measure how well they can navigate our software. And by navigate I mean being able to inspect data, call right tools, change widget parameters, build a dashboard, add new widgets on the dashboard, leverage skills, etc..

This has lead me to build [WorkspaceBench](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench).

It includes the harness, a deterministic workspace simulator, 300 different synthethically generated tasks, graders, and everything else. The [Stark Industries mock enterprise apps](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/stark-industries-demo), which I used for the demo above, is where the benchmark's tasks come from — the same widgets and apps, turned into tasks.

One warning before we start, because it shaped everything below: AI-generated evals are easy to produce and hard to make sound. If you don't keep going back to the underlying data — traces, per-check results, run-to-run flips — you are building on a weak foundation. OpenAI's [Separating signal from noise in coding evaluations](https://openai.com/index/separating-signal-from-noise-coding-evaluations/) makes this point well, and it matches everything I ran into here.

## Why a benchmark {#why}

The workspace was built on a lot of feedback from users (and a few millions in R&D). An agent driving it over [Workspace MCP](https://openbb.co/blog/introducing-workspace-mcp/) leaves (kinda) similar traces to a person and acts on the same durable state - the dashboards, widgets, and apps.

But the feedback loop is different.

Humans drive with a mouse, and you can infer a lot from it: hesitation, rage clicks, even aha moments. When I see how a user utilizes the workspace, I can immediately spot paper cuts or ways to improve the product, without the user even raising these. And then I can always ask the user directly about their experience - well, not always, but often!

An agent doesn't give me this. It won't hover around like a human would and I can't ask that agent what it was trying to do at that time. Although, the actions (via MCP tools) are legible, they state exactly what it's doing, but without knowing what it was trying to accomplish, I can't tell the overall goal.

A benchmark fixes that half: I wrote the task, so intent is known by construction. And once intent is pinned down, I can run multiple rollouts (from different models) to understand how they get that task done; and as a byproduct where they succeed or fail.

There are many benches in the wild. Some relevant ones:

- [SWE-bench](https://www.swebench.com/) ([Jimenez et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.06770)) — resolve a real GitHub issue, graded on whether the patch passes the tests.
- [Terminal-Bench](https://www.tbench.ai/) — finish a task in a shell, graded on what actually happened.
- [WebArena](https://webarena.dev/) ([Zhou et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.13854)) — reach a goal across real websites, verified programmatically.
- [OSWorld](https://os-world.github.io/) ([Xie et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.07972)) — full desktop tasks across real applications.

Each of these allows labs to represent via a single number how good their model is at that specific domain. (Debatable if this is a good thing or not, particularly with test-time scaling)

With WorkspaceBench I'm trying to assess how a model can operate our financial workspace with the MCP tools we offer.

A few of the things I'm looking to get out of this:

- **Finding workspace bugs.** Our own test suite catches most of those already, so I don't expect to run into many here - but you never know.
- **Where the tool surface can be improved** — the schemas, names, and error messages they models trip on. This is the biggest lever, because fixing the MCP surface once makes every agent better at the same time. At the same time you don't want to overfit the tools to a small 8b model, as this can impact performance of a bigger model.
- **Which models I can honestly recommend.** When a user asks which agents can drive their workspace best, I'd love to be able to point to some hard data rather than vibes.
- **What to train on.** With every run we have a full graded trace, and those traces could potentially be used to post-train a small, cheap model to drive the workspace. Could we push to HuggingFace a small agent that clients can run in their own environment to navigate their workspace.

Then, this also can work as a way to catch regressions as the product surface increases with new features, or we add more MCP tools.

### A chat answer is very limiting

For financial workflows, chat interface will often be enough. But not always. A lot of times you need to see artifacts that are representing real data in the backend that you can trust, or comparing two time-series side by side, or check how your portfolio is doing overall, or just look into numbers and letting your human brain do pattern recognition on its own.

If you work from that assumption, then you want to measure how the AI can manipulate your workspace state and not the tokens output. For instance - did the widget land on the right dashboard, under the. correct tab, with the right ticker and parameters, without breaking the layout that already existed?

So WorkspaceBench grades the final state, deterministically (i.e. exact widget identifiers, exact data arguments, layout bounds, overlap checks). [τ-bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.12045) takes the same position for tool agents: grade the state, not the chat output - there are other benchmarks for that, and it will be highly dependent on the workflow you are interested in (imo each firm should actually have their own private evals to measure models based on what actually matters to them).

So in a nutshell:

> WorkspaceBench measures whether an agent can operate the workspace, not whether it can reason about markets or financial data.

## Setup {#setup}

### The six models

I picked the following models for testing:

- **GPT-5.5** — the most advanced model available
- **Claude Sonnet 5** — a recent release, and fairly affordable (and more affordable than Claude Fable 5)
- **GLM-5.2** — the best open-weights model right now
- **gpt-4.1-mini** — a solid, cheap model, and what I used as the gating model to calibrate the levels (more on this later)
- **gpt-oss:20b** — a strong open-weights mixture-of-experts
- **Qwen3 8B** — a small open-weights dense model

The last two run on my own machine through Ollama for free; the rest go through vendor APIs or OpenRouter.

### Workspace MCP tools

[Workspace MCP](https://openbb.co/blog/introducing-workspace-mcp/) is the server that turns OpenBB Workspace into something an agent can operate. It exposes the workspace (dashboards, widgets, data, apps, skills) as a set of MCP tools and resources, so a model can list what exists, read a widget's schema, create and update widgets, lay them out, and assemble whole apps.

What an LLM can access over MCP is a subset of what a person can do in the workspace, since a person can click around the interface.

As a matter of fact, a coding agent like Codex can use the in-app browser, and Claude Code can use the /chrome tool to navigate the browser, but I am ignoring this. So whatever the scores are in this post - treat them as a lower bound of what an agent can really do navigating the workspace. I'm trying to evaluate the raw model (without including any harness).

The benchmark can't call the real hosted MCP server on every run - that wouldn't scale well. So I shared the Workspace MCP code (which we actually had open source [here](https://github.com/OpenBB-finance/workspace-mcp)) with my coding agent so it could build a simulator. A deterministic state machine that has the exact same tool contract - tool names, argument shapes and error responses. And to make sure the simulator doesn't quietly drift from the real thing, a smoke runner can replay any task through a real local `workspace-mcp` sidecar and diff the advertised tool surface, so a change to the server shows up as a failure instead of the benchmark grading a workspace that no longer exists.

The Workspace MCP has:
- **2 prompts** - the server's own usage guidance and session context, fetched with `get_workspace_prompt`
- **MCP resources** - the app-builder index and the workspace skills, read with `read_workspace_resource`
- **20 tools** - the full read-and-write surface: discovery (`get_workspace_snapshot`, `list_available_widgets`, `get_widget_schema`), widget CRUD (`create_widget`, `update_widget`, `update_widget_layout`, `delete_widget`), navigation (`navigate_workspace`, `manage_dashboard`), the higher-level surface (`manage_apps`, `get_skill_content`, `assign_tasks_to_agents`), and a few more you can see in the chart below

Every bar below counts how many of the 300 tasks require that part of the surface. Nothing goes untested - every tool is needed by at least 20 tasks - but obviously some are more popular than others.

### Fifteen families of workspace work

Every task is anchored on one MCP capability — its **family**. There are 15 of them, 20 tasks each, and grouped they cover five kinds of workspace work:

- **Widget CRUD** — `create`, `update`, `delete`, `layout`: place, change, remove, and arrange widgets on the grid.
- **Reading & recording** — `read`, `inspect`, `note`: pull the data behind widgets, reason about a workspace the agent didn't build, leave durable notes behind.
- **Navigation & precision** — `navigate`, `params`: move across dashboards and tabs; parameter surgery on existing widgets.
- **Platform leverage** — `apps`, `skills`, `delegate`, `backends`: instantiate app templates, run workspace skills, delegate to other agents, manage data backends. (Instantiate, not create - building an app from scratch is what the [`build-openbb-apps` suite](#build-openbb-apps) below tests.)
- **Protocol surface** — `resources`, `prompts`: the MCP resources and prompts most agents never think to read.

### Five levels of difficulty

Families define which capability a task exercises, on the other hand levels tries to convey its difficulty via what the task is made of - i.e. how many tool calls it requires, whether the starting state is seeded with something wrong, how much discovery is required, and how tight the turn budget is.

Here they are:
- **t0 — single action.** One tool call does the job.
- **t1 — one action and some reasoning.** E.g. discover the catalog, fetch the schema, then act.
- **t2 — composed artifacts.** Several widgets, tabs, and a note have to land together as one coherent dashboard.
- **t3 — repair & preserve.** The episode starts on a populated workspace: sometimes something is seeded broken to fix, always something healthy that must survive untouched.
- **t4 — multi-intent composition.** Several of the above in a single episode, under a specific turn budget.

Here is what repair & preserve looks like in practice — an agent (not a raw model in this case!) correcting a wrong ticker while leaving the healthy widgets untouched:

### How the 300 tasks are generated

Every task is AI-generated including the early ones I drafted myself.

I got inspired by the approach taken by [TMax](https://wai-org.com/blog/tmax/) which sampled 14,600 terminal environments compositionally from structured axes.

So the suite is generated compositionally: **15 families, each anchored on one MCP capability, each carrying a complete t0–t4 ladder** - 4 tasks per family, 300 in total.

The 4 per cell gives each task variety (different widgets, tickers, backends, prompts) and lets the boundary levels split difficulty evenly (t1 is 2 easy + 2 medium, t3 is 2 medium + 2 hard). The same tool is tested at every level: `delete_widget` at t0 means "remove the only widget"; at t4 it means "remove one duplicate, fix the survivor, document the repair" in a single episode.

I still didn't like that I didn't have a good view of what tools were required by each of the tasks (apart from looking at the code of course), so I built a tool required viewer that gave me that visibility. Also, click on one of the rows to dig into that specific task!

One more way to read the 300: ignore which tools a task calls and ask what the analyst actually asked for. Every task is one of five jobs - the **workflow levels** (L0–L4) below — from "read this workspace and tell me what's there" to "something here is broken, fix it without wrecking the rest".

A level isn't a third axis I designed; it's read straight off the family × level cell.

Take the `delete` family: at t0–t2 the job is single-widget housekeeping (L1), but at t3 the dashboard has two identical widgets and the ask is "remove exactly one duplicate, then document what you did". Same anchor tool, but the job has become repair (L4). The mix is deliberate: building dashboards (L2) is the most product-relevant job, so it gets a quarter of the suite. And repair earns its slot because agents, and users, make mistakes and need to recover.

## How it runs {#how-it-runs}

The whole benchmark diagram can be seen below (click on each of the steps).

    return <EvalLoopDiagram />;

Follow one task through it:

1. **The task is a JSON file** — the prompt, the fixture backends to seed, the tools the agent may call, and the exact checks that count as success. One file per task, under [`workspace_bench_v1/`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/core/task_suites/workspace_bench_v1/).
2. **The harness** reads it, seeds a deterministic workspace, writes a task envelope to disk, sets four environment variables, and runs whatever command you named.
3. **Your agent** — a Python adapter, a CLI tool, a model behind an API — reads the envelope and emits its actions as JSON-lines tool calls. It never imports the benchmark.
4. **The simulator** ([`simulated_workspace.py`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/workspace/simulated_workspace.py)) applies each call and hands back a result, turn by turn.
5. **Everything is saved** — each run directory keeps the full `conversation.json`, `model_responses.jsonl`, and `tool_calls.jsonl`, and the whole run is exported as portable rollout JSONL in [`runs/exports/`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/runs/exports/) (1,800 episodes committed there already).
6. **The grader** ([`graders.py`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/core/graders.py)) reads the final workspace state first, then the trace, and emits pass/fail, a score, and stable issue codes.

<br />

Every edge in that loop is a plain, boring shape — a task file in, tool-call JSONL out, a graded scorecard — which is exactly why any agent, in any language, plugs in without importing a thing. Click any stage in the diagram above to see the file it lives in and an example of what flows through it.

Running the whole thing is then one command, and re-certifying it is another:

```bash
# run any adapter file against the full 300-task core suite
uv run workspace-bench --models-file models.json --suite core

# re-certify the suite: oracle passes, no-op fails, quotas + fingerprints hold
uv run workspace-bench validate --suite core --min-tasks 300

# export a finished run as portable rollout JSONL
uv run workspace-bench export-rollouts \
  --comparison-dir runs/comparison/<run> --output rollouts.jsonl
```

The simulator is intentional: it is not a pixel or browser simulator, it is a deterministic state machine for the Workspace MCP contract, which makes runs fast, reproducible, and cheap enough for CI. A live smoke runner ([`live_mcp.py`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/workspace/live_mcp.py)) drives the same task contract through a real local `workspace-mcp` sidecar, so drift from the real server shows up as a failure instead of going unnoticed.

## Certifying the benchmark {#calibration}

A level system only makes sense if, well, there are levels. So in this case I wanted to check whether with each difficulty level, the models were struggling more and more. This would allow me to calibrate the difficulty axis empirically.

So I picked a cheap mid-strength model (gpt-4.1-mini) and run it over all 300 tasks, to check whether the pass rate would fall monotonically from t0 to t4. And they did, very nicely as a matter of fact!

### Synthetic test selection

When you generate tasks instead of hand-writing them, you cannot trust any single one by default given that the generator will produce broken tasks, unsolvable tasks, and tasks that are ridiculously trivial. This is the problem [TMax](https://wai-org.com/blog/tmax/) ran into at 14,600 environments and solves with a pipeline: generate, then put every candidate through machine checks before it is allowed into the suite.

WorkspaceBench borrows that idea.

<BrowserOnly>
  {() => {
    const { CalibrationLoopDiagram } = require('@site/src/components/WorkspaceBench/InteractiveDashboard');
    return <CalibrationLoopDiagram />;
  }}
</BrowserOnly>

Each stage does something specific, this is basically "synthetic selection":

- **Generate** — build a candidate task from a family template: which tool it anchors on, which level, which pathologies to seed.
- **Certify** — two mechanical checks every task has to pass.
   - *Oracle passes*: the reference solution - i.e. the known-good sequence of tool calls - is replayed through the grader and must pass, which proves the task is actually solvable.
   - *No-op fails*: an agent that does nothing is graded and must fail, which proves the task isn't already satisfied by the starting workspace (no free passes).
- **Gate** — run the calibration model and check its pass rate lands in the band the level claims: high at t0, low at t4.
- **Fix the generator** — if a whole level or family comes out wrong, the bug is in the template, not in one task. Fix it there and re-run the loop over all 300.

<br />

Let me add some color on how this **did indeed work**:

The first gating run came back suspicious: the two families anchored on the benchmark's two newest tools scored exactly 0/20. Pulling on that thread surfaced three real defects - all in the benchmark, none in the models:

1. The harness never documented the two new tools. `get_workspace_prompt` and `read_workspace_resource` had no schema reference, so every model invented argument shapes and got rejected call after call. That alone zeroed both families.
2. Some prompts asked for less than the grader checked. One inspect task graded the note for mentioning the series id DGS10, but the prompt never asked for it. The model did what it was told and still failed.
3. Schema discipline was specified inconsistently. Some prompts said "fetch the schema before creating", others silently expected it and failed the model for skipping it.

<br />

All three got fixed at the generator level, not by patching individual tasks, then the suite was re-certified and the sweep re-run from scratch. The gating curve went from a broken 83–67–48–47–30 to the clean **98–92–77–52–33** above.

One last thing about the gating model, since it is the only one I ran more than once: I ran gpt-4.1-mini three times over all 300 to check the numbers were stable.

| **Repeatability** — gpt-4.1-mini × 3 |  |
| --- | --- |
| attempt spread | **71.7% · 70.0% · 70.7%** strict across three independent attempts. A 1.7-point spread, so one attempt is good to about ±1 point overall |
| level stability | pooled per-level pass rates (98 · 93 · 77 · 55 · 31) match the single-attempt curve within ±3 points, and the curve is monotonic in all three attempts |
| flaky tasks | **19 of 300** pass sometimes and fail sometimes for the same model - 1 · 0 · 3 · 5 · 10 across t0→t4. The flakiness concentrates exactly where the ladder puts difficulty |
| capability vs reliability | pass@3 **73.7%** vs pass^3 **67.3%**. The 6.4-point gap between "passed at least once" and "passed every time" is what reliability costs |

Every other model in this post is a single attempt, then: a point estimate good to about a point, assuming its variance looks anything like mini's.

Two things I read off it before anything else.

The second is the gap between the two scoring rows. There is also an inversion hiding in there: GLM-5.2's mean check score (90.1%) sits below gpt-4.1-mini's (95.7%) and even Qwen3 8B's (90.8%) despite a much higher strict pass rate. GLM fails messily - when it blows a task it takes several checks down with it - while the weaker models fail narrowly.

The family block at the bottom is where I spend most of my time. Read down a column and you get a model's failure fingerprint; read across the inspect row and you see the suite's open problem.

And one thing I learned while getting these runs fair: adapter plumbing can cost a fronlevel model double-digit points. OpenRouter can route Anthropic models through Bedrock, and requesting `json_schema` `response_format` on that route degraded Sonnet 5 to schema-minimal outputs. A controlled A/B caught it, and the fix was in my harness - disable `response_format` on that route - not in the model.

For anyone who wants to reproduce the table, this is the test of record:

- **Release**: one release, 300 tasks, certified before any model ran (oracle 300/300, no-op 0/300).
- **Models**: gpt-5.5 & gpt-4.1-mini (OpenAI API) · anthropic/claude-sonnet-5 & z-ai/glm-5.2 (OpenRouter) · gpt-oss:20b & qwen3:8b (local Ollama).
- **Protocol**: [pass@1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.03374) - one attempt per task, interactive tool loop, temperature 0 (GPT-5.5: 1, API-enforced), same turn budget and grader for every model.
- **Artifacts**: 1,800 full episode transcripts as rollout JSONL in `runs/exports/`, plus the compiled report in `runs/reports/calibration.json`.
- **Cost**: ≈ $60 of API spend across the calibration loop and the final sweep, run July 2026.

## Expanding the benchmark: build-openbb-apps {#build-openbb-apps}

### Motivation {#build-motivation}

After validating the first 300 tasks, one gap kept bothering me. Everything above measures an agent *operating* a workspace that already exists - but the thing I actually want agents to do next is understand `widgets.json` and `apps.json` well enough to **build apps from scratch on top of a user's data API**. That is a different capability, and nothing in the original suite touches it.

I could have gone back and reworked the 300. I deliberately didn't. Instead, the benchmark is architected as **suites**: certified sets of tasks that get added independently, reported separately, and rolled up into one aggregate view. The original 300 became the `core` suite - operating the workspace; the tasks below are **`build-openbb-apps`**. And the reason this matters beyond my own roadmap: any firm can extend the bench with a suite built from the workflows that matter to *them* - workspace skills, macro workflows, client advisory, research - same task schema, same certification gates, same reporting. `build-openbb-apps` is both a new capability axis and proof that the architecture actually extends (which was kind of the bet).

### Building apps, not instantiating them {#build-vs-instantiate}

There is a distinction the core suite papers over. Its `apps` family *instantiates* app templates - and instantiating is genuinely useful, but it is one tool call that opens an existing template into a dashboard. Nothing gets created: no data is wired, no widget is defined, the app was already there.

Building an app is a different job entirely. In the real product an app IS a backend: a `widgets.json` (which widgets exist - their endpoints, params, columns, grid sizes) and an `apps.json` (which widgets land on which tabs, at which coordinates, wired to which shared parameters), served over HTTP against real data endpoints. This is the part that matters for enterprises: an agent that can write those two files can turn a firm's internal data API into a working workspace app. And it isn't a benchmark invention - the real Workspace MCP server ships sixteen app-building resources over the protocol (the widgets.json and apps.json specs, a build-an-app guide, a common-errors catalog) - agents building backends is exactly where the product is going.

So `build-openbb-apps` makes the deliverable the definition files themselves. Not code - I am not testing whether a model writes FastAPI routes. "Valid" means what the workspace frontend means by it: I transcribed the production validation rules into the simulator (required fields, the real 19-value type enum, the param type system, column rules, app schema, every referenced widget id must actually be served), and everything flows through the same tool that connects backends in real life - `manage_backends operation='add'` carries the payloads your backend would serve, `operation='refresh'` re-submits them, so repair works like real life: fix your backend, hit refresh.

### What one build task looks like {#build-one-task}

Here's a level-2 task from the `aggrid` family, verbatim except for trimming. Note what it does *not* contain: any JSON.

> Register "Earnings Prep Data" (http://localhost:7805) with a single table — `revision_grid`: name "Revision Grid", description "Street revision momentum by ticker.", endpoint /revision-momentum, type ssrm_table, gridData w=20 h=9. Requirements: it is served as a server-side row-model grid whose rows are read from the response key "rows"; and the columns come from the served rows `[{"momentum_pct": 0.084, "revised_down": 3, "revised_up": 14, "ticker": "AAPL"}, ...]`. Derive the table columns using the workspace conventions: every field becomes a column; headerName is the field name with underscores as spaces, Title Case; cellDataType is number for numeric values, dateString for ISO dates, text otherwise; fields ending in `_pct` additionally carry formatterFn percent.

The deliverable is the definition itself. To pass, the model has to produce — through `manage_backends`, surviving real validation — exactly this:

```json
"revision_grid": {
  "name": "Revision Grid",
  "description": "Street revision momentum by ticker.",
  "endpoint": "/revision-momentum",
  "type": "ssrm_table",
  "gridData": { "w": 20, "h": 9 },
  "data": {
    "dataKey": "rows",
    "table": {
      "columnsDefs": [
        { "field": "ticker", "headerName": "Ticker", "cellDataType": "text" },
        { "field": "revised_up", "headerName": "Revised Up", "cellDataType": "number" },
        { "field": "revised_down", "headerName": "Revised Down", "cellDataType": "number" },
        { "field": "momentum_pct", "headerName": "Momentum Pct",
          "cellDataType": "number", "formatterFn": "percent" }
      ]
    }
  }
}
```

The grader then checks that every stated field landed, that the four columns were *derived* correctly from the sample rows (Title-Case headers, the percent formatter on `_pct`), and that the response key is wired via `data.dataKey` — the exact failure that separates the mid-field from the fronlevel on this suite. Every graded value is stated in the brief, derivable from a stated convention, or readable from the backend; nothing has to be guessed.

### The shape of the suite {#build-shape}

**212 tasks: 10 families × the t0-t4 ladder × 4 per cell, plus a 12-task `e2e` capstone that exists only at t4** - there is no structurally trivial version of "ship a whole app". One JSON file per task, under [`workspace_bench_v2_build_openbb_apps/`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/core/task_suites/workspace_bench_v2_build_openbb_apps/).

The families are not something I invented. The [backend-examples repo](https://github.com/OpenBB-finance/backend-examples-for-openbb-workspace) ships an onboarding app for developers where each tab demonstrates one widget capability - basic content types, configuration settings, parameters, forms, AG Grid tables, charts, advanced widgets, parameter grouping. That's basically the official map of what building a widget breaks down into, so I just adopted it: eight widget-side families, one per onboarding tab, plus two app-side families (`apps` - assembling widgets into dashboards - and `extend` - modifying a live backend without breaking it) and the capstone. Coverage is enforced by construction: every one of the **16 widget types** and every param type is *owned* by exactly one family, and generation fails if a family stops building its types. Fixture data is neutral market/ops material - eight desks, ~45 endpoints - because the benchmark is about the schema system, not any particular firm's domain.

The ladder measures one thing: how much of the app the model has to build, and how much of the spec it has to *derive* rather than copy.

- **t0 - one widget, the right schema.** A requirements brief in words; the model writes a single widget definition. The floor rung, deliberately nearly free.
- **t1 - ship it as an app.** The same class of widget, now wrapped in a one-tab `apps.json` that references it - and graded in full, widget *and* app. That containment is deliberate: t1 is t0's skill plus one more, so t1 can never score above t0 except by noise.
- **t2 - composed requirements.** Still one artifact, but at least three requirement dimensions at once, and at least one is a *policy*, not a value: the brief says "cache results for 15 minutes" and the model has to know that means `staleTime: 900000`, or the columns have to be derived from the sample rows the backend serves. This is the rung where transcription stops being enough.
- **t3 - the app takes shape.** Two to three widgets across a multi-tab app, plus a graded composition dimension - a shared parameter group wired across widgets, or a desk convention ("cached widgets on this desk cache for 15 minutes") that determines values the brief never states per-widget.
- **t4 - operate what you built.** Build t3-weight content, publish it, instantiate the app into a live dashboard, *configure* it (push a parameter value into the running widget), and document the handoff. Crucially, t4 grades only its marginal skill: the widgets that earlier levels already tested in full are graded as light anchors, and the new skill - the operate chain - carries the task.

### Certifying the suite {#build-certification}

Every task clears the same gates as the core suite: the reference solution passes (oracle 212/212), a do-nothing agent fails (no-op 0/212), fingerprints and task ids are unique, type-ownership quotas hold, and - the lesson that survived from my first attempt at this suite - **no task may exceed its level's graded-check cap**. Strict pass compounds per-check accuracy over the number of graded checks (pass ≈ q^N), so a level that silently grades more fields measures grading breadth, not capability. I learned that the expensive way: an early version of this suite graded 9 checks at t0 and 37 at t3, mostly by re-grading every field of every preserved definition, and the level curve tracked check counts instead of skill. The fix is structural and permanent: preserved artifacts grade as three-check anchors, repaired entries grade only their patched field, full grading is reserved for each task's focus artifact, and the certifier fails the build if any task exceeds its cap.

Then the ladder has to earn its labels empirically - and this time I changed the discipline. Gating runs cost money, so instead of burning them while the tasks were still moving, I calibrated against a free local proxy - gpt-oss:20b on Ollama, all 212 tasks, as many rounds as it took - and only promoted the suite to the paid gating model once the proxy staircase was strictly decreasing with no tied levels. The proxy is a *weaker* model than the gate, which is exactly why this works: defects in the tasks show up as violations of a shape you know must hold - a level passing above the level below it, a family flat from t0 to t4 - regardless of which model produces the curve.

Eight proxy rounds, and every one caught a real defect. In the benchmark, not the model. Some fixes were mechanical - the invalid-call budget now scales with payload size, and policy derivation replaced a t2 cell that turned out to be a dictation test - but three of them are worth telling properly:

- **A check the brief never stated.** Round one: every t1 task in one family failed on a single field (`allowCustomization`) that the grader demanded and no brief mentioned. The fairness invariant this produced is now structural: every graded value must be stated in the brief, derivable from a stated convention, or readable from the backend.
- **A free level that was actually free.** t0 sat at 100% for three rounds because a words-form brief alone let the model ship near-empty definitions. t0 now requires a structural floor - at least two schema elements beyond the identity fields.
- **Families with material in the wrong levels.** The aggregate staircase can look right while individual families are broken inside it: one family had its heaviest content at t0, another was flat at 95% across all five levels. The per-family × per-level ladder matrix that caught this is now a permanent certification instrument - within every family, each level must build material at least as heavy as the level below.

The result of that loop, on the proxy: **92.5 · 82.5 · 77.5 · 55.0 · 30.8** - strictly decreasing, no ties, and the two joints that kept inverting mid-calibration (t2→t3, t3→t4) ended up as the biggest steps. Only then did the paid sweep run.

The sweep caught four more defects the proxy couldn't see - and each was found by a *different* model failing in a way the others didn't, which is the strongest argument I know for gating with more than one model:

- The gating model went 0/20 on the forms family, every episode the same shape: guess `submitEndpoint` for the form's submit key, get rejected, never recover. The validator wanted `endpoint` - and its rejection message was the one place in the codebase that didn't name the required key. (The fronlevel models guessed right, which is why the proxy rounds never surfaced it.)
- All four of Sonnet 5's t4-forms failures hit one identical check. The brief rendered a form's inputs and the widget's other params in a single semicolon list, so the configure-target param grammatically read as one of the form's inputs - and Sonnet parsed it exactly that way. Two fronlevel models making the same parse is a brief defect, not a capability gap; form inputs are now bracketed.
- Fourteen of GPT-5.5's fifteen failures weren't graded failures at all: it emitted a second JSON object after its action and the adapter killed the episode. Five of those were e2e capstones. Invalid tool calls got teaching rejections and a budget; malformed responses got the death penalty - inconsistent, now fixed (lenient first-object parse, then a teaching turn).
- Both fronlevel models failed one task on the identical check: `refetchInterval: false`, derived from "do not poll HTTP again" - a mapping no stated convention covered, where omitting the key is a perfectly valid reading. The policy clause now states the mapping.
- And the gate's own curve caught the last one: t0 came in *below* t1. The cause was a grading shortcut - t1 graded its widget as a light three-check anchor ("the skill was already proven at t0"), so t1 actually checked *less* of the widget than t0 did, and a model that fumbles widget details but wraps apps cleanly could outscore its own floor. t1 now grades the full widget plus the app - containment, so each rung provably contains the one below - and every existing episode was regraded through the deterministic simulator, no API call needed.

Every fix was surface- or wording-level - the suite's structure never moved after the proxy froze it. And once the last fix landed, the board below was produced the only way a clean report should be: **one fresh end-to-end run per model over all 212 final tasks** - no patched records, no spliced re-runs. Run any of these models against the repo today and you run exactly what produced its number.

## Conclusion {#conclusion}

I set out to answer one question: if agents are going to drive OpenBB Workspace for real users, how well do they actually do it? WorkspaceBench turns that from a vibe into a number. Six agents, one grader, 300 certified tasks that span single actions up to multi-intent repairs — and a result specific enough to act on. The fronlevel has essentially solved composition (GPT-5.5 is as strong at t4 as at t0) but not precision or ambient-state reasoning; the mid-field separates cleanly; and 15 tasks that nobody passes mark exactly where the ceiling is.

The point of a number is that it moves. Now every change — a clearer tool schema, a newer model, a fine-tune — has somewhere to show up, and I can hill-climb it instead of shipping a demo and hoping. That is the thing I was missing: a way to measure workspace-agent performance, and then tell both the product and our users what to do about it. And the suite no longer stops at driving: the `build-openbb-apps` suite adds 212 tasks — 512 across the two suites — and the same six-model board now covers *building* the workspace, not just operating it. The result reads as a capability profile: the fronlevel has fully solved schema building (GPT-5.5 posts a perfect 212/212 - the first anywhere in the benchmark - with GLM-5.2 and Sonnet 5 right behind) while still leaving points on ambient state in the core suite, and the pooled board separates the models by *which* of the two jobs they can do, not just how well.

## Future work {#future}

Every failure here points at something to fix, in two directions, and both reuse this same benchmark unchanged.

One is the **tool surface**. Look at the failure analysis again: GLM-5.2 tripped the invalid-call budget on dozens of tasks, and `schema_not_called_before_create` dominates the mid-field. Those are not only verdicts on the models; they are verdicts on the MCP tools — the schemas, the names, the error messages the workspace hands to agents. The same graded run that ranks the models is a to-do list for the server: make the surface clearer and every agent gets better at once.

The other is the **agents themselves**. Every episode leaves a full, graded trace — 1,800 of them are already in the repo. A grader that emits a clean pass/fail signal is exactly what a reinforcement-learning loop needs for reward, and the same deterministic simulator can serve as the environment. Turning these traces into training signal for smaller or cheaper agents is the obvious next step.

And the most immediate item: a t5. A perfect score and two 99s on `build-openbb-apps` mean the words-form build ladder is solved at the fronlevel — the headroom that remains lives in the rows the mid-field fails (column derivation from data, the build-publish-instantiate-configure chain) and in the core suite's ambient-state families that even GPT-5.5 doesn't clear. Those failing rows are the raw material for the next suite: harder briefs, longer chains, and specs the model has to *discover* from the workspace's own MCP resources instead of being handed.

Both are open work. For now the point stands on its own: if agents are going to drive a financial workspace, you have to be able to measure how well they do it — and then the measurement tells you what to improve. Incentives always win. The least I can do is measure the ones I am creating.
