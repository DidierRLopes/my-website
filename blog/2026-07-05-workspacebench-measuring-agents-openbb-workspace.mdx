If agents are going to drive this workspace for real analysts, we (OpenBB) need to find a way to measure how well they can navigate our software. And by navigate I mean being able to inspect data, call right tools, change widget parameters, build a dashboard, add new widgets on the dashboard, leverage skills, etc..

This has lead me to build [WorkspaceBench](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench).

It includes the harness, a deterministic workspace simulator, 300 different synthethically generated scenarios, graders, and everything else. The [Stark Industries mock enterprise apps](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/stark-industries-demo), which I used for the demo above, is where the benchmark's scenarios come from — the same widgets and apps, turned into tasks.

## Why a benchmark {#why}

The workspace was built on a lot of feedback from users (and a few millions in R&D). An agent driving it over [Workspace MCP](https://openbb.co/blog/introducing-workspace-mcp/) leaves (kinda) similar traces to a person and acts on the same durable state - the dashboards, widgets, and apps.

But the feedback loop is different.

Humans drive with a mouse, and you can infer a lot from it: hesitation, rage clicks, even aha moments. When I see how a user utilizes the workspace, I can immediately spot paper cuts or ways to improve the product, without the user even raising these. And then I can always ask the user directly about their experience - well, not always, but often!

An agent doesn't give me this. It won't hover around like a human would and I can't ask that agent what it was trying to do at that time. Although, the actions (via MCP tools) are legible, they state exactly what it's doing, but without knowing what it was trying to accomplish, I can't tell the overall goal.

A benchmark fixes that half: I wrote the task, so intent is known by construction. And once intent is pinned down, I can run multiple rollouts (from different models) to understand how they get that task done; and as a byproduct where they succeed or fail.

There are many benches in the wild. Some relevant ones:

- [SWE-bench](https://www.swebench.com/) ([Jimenez et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.06770)) — resolve a real GitHub issue, graded on whether the patch passes the tests.
- [Terminal-Bench](https://www.tbench.ai/) — finish a task in a shell, graded on what actually happened.
- [WebArena](https://webarena.dev/) ([Zhou et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.13854)) — reach a goal across real websites, verified programmatically.
- [OSWorld](https://os-world.github.io/) ([Xie et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.07972)) — full desktop tasks across real applications.

Each of these allows labs to represent via a single number how good their model is at that specific domain. (Debatable if this is a good thing or not, particularly with test-time scaling)

With WorkspaceBench I'm trying to assess how a model can operate our financial workspace with the MCP tools we offer.

A few of the things I'm looking to get out of this:

- **Finding workspace bugs.** Our own test suite catches most of those already, so I don't expect to run into many here - but you never know.
- **Where the tool surface can be improved** — the schemas, names, and error messages they models trip on. This is the biggest lever, because fixing the MCP surface once makes every agent better at the same time. At the same time you don't want to overfit the tools to a small 8b model, as this can impact performance of a bigger model.
- **Which models I can honestly recommend.** When a user asks which agents can drive their workspace best, I'd love to be able to point to some hard data rather than vibes.
- **What to train on.** With every run we have a full graded trace, and those traces could potentially be used to post-train a small, cheap model to drive the workspace. Could we push to HuggingFace a small agent that clients can run in their own environment to navigate their workspace.

Then, this also can work as a way to catch regressions as the product surface increases with new features, or we add more MCP tools.

### A chat answer is very limiting

For financial workflows, chat interface will often be enough. But not always. A lot of times you need to see artifacts that are representing real data in the backend that you can trust, or comparing two time-series side by side, or check how your portfolio is doing overall, or just look into numbers and letting your human brain do pattern recognition on its own.

If you work from that assumption, then you want to measure how the AI can manipulate your workspace state and not the tokens output. For instance - did the widget land on the right dashboard, under the. correct tab, with the right ticker and parameters, without breaking the layout that already existed?

So WorkspaceBench grades the final state, deterministically (i.e. exact widget identifiers, exact data arguments, layout bounds, overlap checks). [τ-bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.12045) takes the same position for tool agents: grade the state, not the chat output - there are other benchmarks for that, and it will be highly dependent on the workflow you are interested in (imo each firm should actually have their own private evals to measure models based on what actually matters to them).

So in a nutshell:

> WorkspaceBench measures whether an agent can operate the workspace, not whether it can reason about markets or financial data.

## Setup {#setup}

### The six models

I picked the following models for testing:

- **GPT-5.5** — the most advanced model available
- **Claude Sonnet 5** — a recent release, and fairly affordable (and more affordable than Fable)
- **GLM-5.2** — the best open-weights model right now
- **gpt-4.1-mini** — a solid, cheap model, and what I used as the gating model to calibrate the tiers (more on this later)
- **gpt-oss:20b** — a strong open-weights mixture-of-experts
- **Qwen3 8B** — a small open-weights dense model

The last two run on my own machine through Ollama for free; the rest go through vendor APIs or OpenRouter.

### Workspace MCP tools

[Workspace MCP](https://openbb.co/blog/introducing-workspace-mcp/) is the server that turns OpenBB Workspace into something an agent can operate. It exposes the workspace (dashboards, widgets, data, apps, skills) as a set of MCP tools and resources, so a model can list what exists, read a widget's schema, create and update widgets, lay them out, and assemble whole apps.

What a LLM can access over MCP is a subset of what a person can do in the workspace since a person can click around the interface. 

As a matter of fact, a coding agent like Codex can use in-app browser or Claude Code can use /chrome tool to navigate the browser, but I am ignoring this. So whatever the scores are in this post - treat them as a lower bound of what an agent can really do navigating the workspace. I'm trying to evaluate the raw model (without including any harness).

The benchmark can't call the real hosted MCP server on every run, this wouldn't scale well. So I shared the Workspace MCP code (which we actually had open source [here](https://github.com/OpenBB-finance/workspace-mcp)) with my coding agent so it could build a simulator. A deterministic state machine that has the exact same tool contract - tool names, argument shapes and error responses. And to make sure the simulator doesn't quietly drift from the real thing, a smoke runner drives the same scenarios through a real local `workspace-mcp` sidecar, so a change to the server's surface shows up as a failure instead of the benchmark grading a workspace that no longer exists.

The Workspace MCP has:
- **2 prompts** - the server's own usage guidance and session context, fetched with get_workspace_prompt
- **MCP resources** - the app-builder index and the workspace skills, read with read_workspace_resource.
- **20 tools** - the full read-and-write surface: discovery (get_workspace_snapshot, list_available_widgets, get_widget_schema), widget CRUD (create_widget, update_widget, update_widget_layout, delete_widget), navigation (navigate_workspace, manage_dashboard), and the higher-level surface (manage_apps, get_skill_content, assign_tasks_to_agents)

Every bar below is extracted from the usage required for the benchmark 300 different scenarios. Each part of the surface is exercised by at least 20 scenarios - that floor is enforced at generation time - but obviously some are more popular than others.

### Fifteen families of workspace work

Every scenario is anchored on one MCP capability — its **family**. There are 15 of them, 20 scenarios each, and grouped they cover five kinds of workspace work:

- **Widget CRUD** — `create`, `update`, `delete`, `layout`: place, change, remove, and arrange widgets on the grid.
- **Reading & recording** — `read`, `inspect`, `note`: pull the data behind widgets, reason about a workspace the agent didn't build, leave durable notes behind.
- **Navigation & precision** — `navigate`, `params`: move across dashboards and tabs; parameter surgery on existing widgets.
- **Platform leverage** — `apps`, `skills`, `delegate`, `backends`: instantiate app templates, run workspace skills, delegate to other agents, manage data backends.
- **Protocol surface** — `resources`, `prompts`: the MCP resources and prompts most agents never think to read.

### Five tiers of difficulty

Families say which capability a task exercises; tiers say how structurally hard it is. And tiers are structural on purpose: a tier is defined by what the task is made of — how many moves it composes, whether the starting state is seeded with something wrong, how much discovery it forces, how tight the turn budget is — not by an adjective I picked.

- **t0 — single action.** One tool call does the job.
- **t1 — one action and some reasoning.** E.g. discover the catalog, fetch the schema, then act.
- **t2 — composed artifacts.** Several widgets, tabs, and a note have to land together as one coherent dashboard.
- **t3 — repair & preserve.** The episode starts on a populated workspace: sometimes something is seeded broken to fix, always something healthy that must survive untouched.
- **t4 — multi-intent composition.** Several of the above in a single episode, under a specific turn budget.

Tiers are uniform by construction — 60 scenarios each — so there is no mix to chart; the difficulty ladder is the design, not an outcome. Here is what repair & preserve looks like in practice — an agent correcting a wrong ticker while leaving the healthy widgets untouched:

### How the 300 scenarios are generated

Every scenario is generated — including the early ones I drafted myself, which were AI-written too, so pretending they were a special hand-audited caste would be dishonest. What matters is not who typed the JSON; it is whether the distribution is designed. My first pack quietly skewed: four MCP tools appeared in exactly one scenario each, and half the suite tested the same single-widget motion. The lesson from [TMax](https://wai-org.com/blog/tmax/) — 14,600 terminal environments sampled compositionally from structured axes — is that a task distribution should be a designed property, not an accident of what the author felt like writing.

So the suite is generated compositionally: **15 families, each anchored on one MCP capability, each carrying a complete t0–t4 ladder** — 4 scenarios per rung per family, 300 total. The 4 per cell gives each rung variety (different widgets, tickers, backends, prompts) and lets the boundary tiers split difficulty evenly (t1 is 2 easy + 2 medium, t3 is 2 medium + 2 hard). The same tool is tested at every tier: `delete_widget` at t0 means "remove the only widget"; at t4 it means "remove one duplicate, fix the survivor, document the repair" in a single episode.

The question a flat scenario count hides is which tools each test actually requires, and how that grows with tier. One row per test; an **x** marks each tool the reference solution uses; the family's anchor tool is highlighted. One last way to read the suite: step back from the tools and ask what kind of analyst work the 300 scenarios add up to. Pick a family and climb its ladder, and at some rung the work often changes kind — `delete` is single-widget housekeeping until t3, where removing the duplicate becomes a repair job; `inspect` starts as read-and-answer and ends as repair; `apps` and `delegate` are platform work at every tier. Bucket all 75 cells by the kind of job they amount to and you get five **workflow levels** (L0–L4): inspect & answer, single widget, build a dashboard, apps & skills, and repair (that last one exists because agents, and users, make mistakes and need to recover from them). This is what the 300 compose into in workflow terms, and the mix is deliberate: dashboard construction (L2) is the most product-relevant slice, so it gets a quarter of the suite.

The one collision worth spelling out is L4 repair vs t3 repair & preserve. L4 says the *job* is repair — the analyst asked for something broken to be fixed — and all 32 L4 tasks sit at t3 or t4, because there is no structurally trivial repair. t3 says the *structure* involves pre-existing state under a preservation constraint, and only 20 of its 60 scenarios are actual repairs; the rest are tasks like "add this widget next to the one already there, without disturbing it" — nothing broken, but plenty to break.

Everything ships as a single release: 300 scenarios, all generated the same way, all certified the same way (that's the [certification section](#calibration)), and all equal weight — no hand-audited elite, no filler.

## How it runs, end to end {#how-it-runs}

The whole benchmark is a small loop, and nothing about your agent has to know it exists.

<BrowserOnly>
  {() => {
    const { EvalLoopDiagram } = require('@site/src/components/WorkspaceBench/InteractiveDashboard');
    return <EvalLoopDiagram />;
  }}
</BrowserOnly>

Follow one scenario through it:

1. **The scenario is a JSON file** — the prompt, the fixture backends to seed, the tools the agent may call, and the exact checks that count as success. One file per task, under [`workspace_bench_v1/`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/core/scenario_packs/workspace_bench_v1/).
2. **The harness** reads it, seeds a deterministic workspace, writes a task envelope to disk, sets four environment variables, and runs whatever command you named.
3. **Your agent** — a Python adapter, a CLI tool, a model behind an API — reads the envelope and emits its actions as JSON-lines tool calls. It never imports the benchmark.
4. **The simulator** ([`simulated_workspace.py`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/workspace/simulated_workspace.py)) applies each call and hands back a result, turn by turn.
5. **Everything is saved** — each run directory keeps the full `conversation.json`, `model_responses.jsonl`, and `tool_calls.jsonl`, and the whole run is exported as portable rollout JSONL in [`runs/exports/`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/runs/exports/) (1,800 episodes committed there already).
6. **The grader** ([`graders.py`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/core/graders.py)) reads the final workspace state first, then the trace, and emits pass/fail, a score, and stable issue codes.

Every edge in that loop is a plain, boring shape — a scenario file in, tool-call JSONL out, a graded scorecard — which is exactly why any agent, in any language, plugs in without importing a thing. Click any stage in the diagram above to see the file it lives in and an example of what flows through it.

Running the whole thing is then one command, and re-certifying it is another:

```bash
# run any adapter file against the full 300-scenario release
uv run --extra dev workspace-bench compare-models \
  --models-file models.json --pack all

# re-certify the pack: oracle passes, no-op fails, quotas + fingerprints hold
uv run --extra dev workspace-bench validate --pack all --min-scenarios 300

# export a finished run as portable rollout JSONL
uv run --extra dev workspace-bench export-rollouts \
  --comparison-dir runs/comparison/<run> --output rollouts.jsonl
```

The simulator is intentional: it is not a pixel or browser simulator, it is a deterministic state machine for the Workspace MCP contract, which makes runs fast, reproducible, and cheap enough for CI. A live smoke runner ([`live_mcp.py`](https://github.com/DidierRLopes/openbb-workspace-bench/blob/main/src/workspace_bench/workspace/live_mcp.py)) drives the same scenario contract through a real local `workspace-mcp` sidecar, so the simulator never drifts away from the real server.

## Certifying the benchmark {#calibration}

A tier is only real if the harder tiers are actually harder. So before trusting any score, I calibrated the difficulty axis empirically. Pick one mid-strength model — gpt-4.1-mini, cheap enough to run a lot — run it over all 300 scenarios, and require its pass rate to fall monotonically from t0 to t4. If the curve comes out flat or bumpy, the tier labels are decoration and the generator needs fixing. If it falls cleanly, the tiers are a real ladder and every test in them has earned its label.

When you generate tasks instead of hand-writing them, you cannot trust any single one — the generator will produce broken tasks, unsolvable tasks, and tasks that are secretly trivial. This is the problem [TMax](https://wai-org.com/blog/tmax/) runs into at 14,600 environments and solves with a pipeline: generate, then put every candidate through machine checks before it is allowed into the suite. WorkspaceBench borrows that idea. Each scenario has to clear a gauntlet before it counts, and the whole thing runs as a loop:

Each stage does something specific:

- **Generate** — build a candidate scenario from a family template: which tool it anchors on, which tier, which pathologies to seed.
- **Certify** — two mechanical checks every scenario has to pass. *Oracle passes*: the reference solution — the known-good sequence of tool calls — is replayed through the grader and must pass, which proves the task is actually solvable. *No-op fails*: an agent that does nothing is graded and must fail, which proves the task isn't already satisfied by the starting workspace (no free passes).
- **Gate** — run the calibration model and check its pass rate lands in the band the tier claims: high at t0, low at t4.
- **Fix the generator** — if a whole tier or family comes out wrong, the bug is in the template, not in one task. Fix it there and re-run the loop over all 300.

Nothing enters the benchmark by hand; it enters by surviving.

That loop earned its keep immediately. The first gating run exposed three real defects in my own benchmark: two brand-new families scored 0/20 because the harness never documented the two new tools' schemas to models (so they invented argument shapes), and because several prompts demanded facts the grader checked but the instructions never asked for. A third pass caught an inconsistency in when schema-discipline was instructed versus silently expected. All three were fixed at the generator level, the pack re-certified, and the sweep re-run from scratch — and the gating curve went from a broken 83–67–48–47–30 to the clean **98–92–77–52–33** above. That is the difference between a benchmark and a demo: a demo shows the good runs, a benchmark survives its own generator being wrong.

One last thing about the gating model, since it is the only one I ran more than once: I ran gpt-4.1-mini three times over all 300 to check the numbers are stable, not luck.

| **Repeatability** — gpt-4.1-mini × 3 |  |
| --- | --- |
| attempt spread | **71.7% · 70.0% · 70.7%** strict across three independent attempts — a 1.7-point spread, so a single attempt is a point estimate good to about ±1 point overall |
| tier stability | pooled per-tier pass rates (98 · 93 · 77 · 55 · 31) repeat the single-attempt curve within ±3 points, and the curve is monotonic in every one of the three attempts |
| flappiness | **19 of 300** scenarios pass sometimes and fail sometimes for the same model — 1 · 0 · 3 · 5 · 10 across t0→t4: within-model variance concentrates exactly where the ladder puts difficulty |
| capability vs reliability | pass@3 **73.7%** against pass^3 **67.3%** — the 6.4-point gap between them is what reliability costs, and the strict single-attempt number sits in the middle |

Every other model in this post is a single attempt, then — a point estimate good to about a point, with the tiers stable across repeats.

Two things to read off the table before the charts. First, the spread. 94.0% to 49.7% strict separates roughly two generations of capability, and the ordering is stable: across all 300 scenarios there is exactly 1 scenario where the bottom model beats the top one (a t0 delegation task GPT-5.5 fumbles and Qwen3 8B happens to pass). Second, the gap between mean check score and strict pass: GPT-5.5 earns 99.2% of all grader checks but passes 94.0% of scenarios — partial credit flatters everyone, which is exactly why the headline metric is all-checks-or-nothing. One inversion hides in the mean checks too: GLM-5.2's mean check score (90.1%) sits below gpt-4.1-mini's (95.7%) and even Qwen3 8B's (90.8%) despite a far higher strict pass rate — GLM fails messily across many checks while weaker models fail narrowly. The family block at the bottom is where I spend the most time: read down a column and you get a model's failure fingerprint; read across the inspect row and you see the suite's open problem.

Adapter plumbing can cost a frontier model double-digit points. OpenRouter can route Anthropic models through Bedrock, and requesting `json_schema` `response_format` there degraded Sonnet to schema-minimal outputs; a controlled A/B diagnosed it, and the harness fixed the adapter by disabling `response_format` on that route.

The rows nobody passes are the most valuable output of the whole sweep. 15 of 300 scenarios defeat all six models, and they are not scattered: 8 are inspect-family tasks (deduplicate or reconcile a workspace the agent did not build — ambient-state reasoning), 4 are companion-widget parameter tasks, 2 are precise layout splits, and 1 is a full skills-driven tearsheet. Even GPT-5.5 passes only 11/20 of the inspect family. That list is where a future t5 comes from, and it doubles as a work queue for the tool surface itself. It is also the clearest evidence that the remaining headroom is about reading state, not composing calls. At the opposite end, 93 scenarios are passed by everyone — the sanity floor, 70 of them in t0–t1, which is what "t0 nearly free" should look like.

Failures are also model-specific in ways a single number hides. Qwen3 8B scores 4/20 on the apps family — long instantiation chains — where every other model scores 17 or better. GLM-5.2 is the only model that struggles with the prompts family (8/20), because it skips the prompt fetch it considers unnecessary. gpt-oss:20b bottoms out on documentation notes (5/20). Six models, six different failure fingerprints on identical tasks — which is why the chart below breaks every model out by failure type, instead of averaging the differences away.

> The honest read: the benchmark separates the mid-field sharply, but the frontier is already near the ceiling, and what it still fails is precision and ambient state, not composition. I would treat that as the roadmap for t5.

## Conclusion {#conclusion}

I set out to answer one question: if agents are going to drive OpenBB Workspace for real users, how well do they actually do it? WorkspaceBench turns that from a vibe into a number. Six agents, one grader, 300 certified tasks that span single actions up to multi-intent repairs — and a result specific enough to act on. The frontier has essentially solved composition (GPT-5.5 is as strong at t4 as at t0) but not precision or ambient-state reasoning; the mid-field separates cleanly; and 15 tasks that nobody passes mark exactly where the ceiling is.

The point of a number is that it moves. Now every change — a clearer tool schema, a newer model, a fine-tune — has somewhere to show up, and I can hill-climb it instead of shipping a demo and hoping. That is the thing I was missing: a way to measure workspace-agent performance, and then tell both the product and our users what to do about it.

## Future work {#future}

Every failure here points at something to fix, in two directions, and both reuse this same benchmark unchanged.

One is the **tool surface**. Look at the failure analysis again: GLM-5.2 tripped the invalid-call budget on dozens of scenarios, and `schema_not_called_before_create` dominates the mid-field. Those are not only verdicts on the models; they are verdicts on the MCP tools — the schemas, the names, the error messages the workspace hands to agents. The same graded run that ranks the models is a to-do list for the server: make the surface clearer and every agent gets better at once.

The other is the **agents themselves**. Every episode leaves a full, graded trace — 1,800 of them are already in the repo. A grader that emits a clean pass/fail signal is exactly what a reinforcement-learning loop needs for reward, and the same deterministic simulator can serve as the environment. Turning these traces into training signal for smaller or cheaper agents is the obvious next step.

Both are open work. For now the point stands on its own: if agents are going to drive a financial workspace, you have to be able to measure how well they do it — and then the measurement tells you what to improve. Incentives always win. The least I can do is measure the ones I am creating.
